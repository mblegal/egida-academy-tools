# Lesson-block scenarios - Week 2

## Introduction to Week 2 (Module 2: Earthworks, base course and subgrade preparation)

Week 2 is the bridge between the safety and materials foundation (Week 1) and the actual paving with block pavers (Week 3). This week the trainees learn what really decides the durability of a pavement: correct setting out of the surface, properly executed earthworks, a sound base course, seating of edge restraints and kerbs, and preparation of the bedding layer. The trade truth is simple: even the most beautifully laid block paving cracks, rocks (PL: klawiszuje) and forms ruts if whatever lies beneath it was done badly. That is why this module is the heart of the whole course's quality. The motto of the week: "A good surface starts with a good bottom".

Each day runs about 7 hours of training (excluding breaks) and takes place on an active construction site according to a fixed rhythm: language warm-up and review, theory introduction in the welfare facilities, trainer demonstration, observation of real work on site, supervised hands-on practice, and a discussion of mistakes with a wrap-up. Indicative time split: about 2.5 h of theory, about 3.5 h of observation and practice, about 0.5 h of discussion.

Day 10 closes the module: besides the bedding-layer topic, it includes a review of the whole week and Partial Test no. 2. Throughout the week the trainer keeps practical-observation records for each trainee.

A recurring rule of the week (worth enforcing every day): we work "from the bottom up" and "from the face" (PL: z czola), that is, we do not step onto finished, profiled and compacted layers so as not to disturb them. The second rule: water always has to drain somewhere, so we plan the falls and the direction of drainage before we start digging.

## Day 6: Drawings and measurement - reading the design, levels, falls, setting out

### Block header

- Course day: 6 (Week 2, Module 2: Earthworks, base course and subgrade preparation).
- Topic: Reading simple drawings and the pavement design, levels (PL: rzedne) and falls (PL: spadki), the direction of water drainage, benchmarks, setting out the outline, staking and string lines.
- Duration: about 7 hours of training (excluding breaks).
- Location: active construction site - theory block in the welfare facilities or a separate zone, practical part on the area designated for the pavement.

### Objectives (knowledge, skills, attitudes)

Knowledge: the trainee understands what a level (PL: rzedna), a fall (PL: spadek) and the direction of water drainage are; knows what a benchmark (PL: reper) and a reference point are; understands why a minimum fall of 1-2% is necessary for drainage; knows the basic markings on a simple pavement drawing.

Skills: the trainee can read dimensions and falls from a simple drawing; can stretch mason's string lines, drive in stakes and set out the outline of an area; can check the level with a straightedge and a spirit level and indicate which way the water drains.

Attitudes: accuracy of measurement (a few millimetres of error at the start become centimetres of error at the end), the habit of double-checking ("measure twice, dig once"), cooperation in a pair when stretching the string line.

### Materials, tools and PPE

- Materials and aids: a simple pavement drawing (a plan with dimensions and fall), wooden or steel stakes, mason's string line, chalk or marking spray, nails/pins.
- Measuring tools: tape measure (a 5 m retractable tape and a 20-30 m tape), spirit level (PL: poziomica, libella), mason's straightedge (PL: lata), water level (PL: waz wodny), where available an optical level (PL: niwelator) with a levelling staff or a laser level, builder's square (PL: katownik).
- Auxiliary equipment: a hammer for driving stakes, spacer pegs.
- PPE (personal protective equipment, PL: SOI): safety helmet, work boots with a toecap and an anti-puncture insole, high-visibility vest, work gloves; in sunny weather glasses and head covering.

### Health and safety issues

- Move around the active site only along designated routes, away from working machines and loading zones.
- Drive in stakes carefully to protect hands and knees; mark protruding stakes so that nobody trips or gets impaled on them.
- A string line stretched at leg height is a tripping hazard: mark it with warning tape at crossing points.
- When using a laser level, do not look into the beam; the laser class must comply with the instructions, and the eyes are protected in line with the manufacturer's recommendations.
- Keep the measuring zone tidy at all times: tapes rolled up, nails put away.

### Course of the lesson

1. Language warm-up and review (about 0.5 h): a short review of the Week 1 materials (aggregates, paving blocks). Introduction and practice of the pronunciation of the key words of the day: level, fall, level (flat), string line, stake, set out. The trainer shows an object and says the word, the trainees repeat.
2. Theory introduction (about 2 h): what a pavement drawing is and how to read it; levels and elevation points; fall as the ratio of height to length (1% = 1 cm per 1 m, 2% = 2 cm per 1 m); why water must have somewhere to drain; the concept of a benchmark and a fixed point. Whiteboard exercise: calculating the height difference for a given fall and length.
3. Trainer demonstration (about 1 h): the trainer stretches a string line between stakes, sets the level with a spirit level and straightedge, shows how to give the string line a 2% fall, how to check a right angle with a square and the 3-4-5 method (a triangle with sides of 3, 4 and 5 units gives a right angle).
4. Observation of real work on site (about 1 h): the trainees watch how a crew or a surveyor sets out an area, where the benchmarks are and how levels are transferred. The trainer points out the direction of water drainage on the structure.
5. Supervised hands-on practice (about 2 h): the trainees, working in pairs, set out a simple rectangular outline, drive in stakes, stretch string lines, give a 2% fall and check the right angle. The trainer walks between the workstations and corrects them.
6. Discussion of mistakes and wrap-up (about 0.5 h): a joint check of the outlines set out, a discussion of typical errors (no fall, crooked angles), a reminder of the "measure twice" rule.

### Teaching methods

Demonstration with explanation, exercise in pairs (peer learning, the stronger speaker supports the weaker one), learning by doing, visualisation on the whiteboard and on the structure, step-by-step instruction. Plenty of gestures and "live" showing, little abstraction.

### Assessment and grading

Practical-observation record: whether the trainee correctly read the fall from the drawing, whether they set out a rectangle with a right angle, whether they gave the correct direction and value of the fall. Short oral check questions (where will the water drain, how much is 2% over 4 m). Descriptive grade: meets / needs support / does not meet.

### Key vocabulary

- Level (PL: rzedna) - the height of a given point relative to an adopted reference level.
- Fall (PL: spadek) - the inclination of a surface given as a percentage; it ensures water runoff.
- Benchmark (PL: reper) - a fixed, unchanging elevation point from which we measure.
- Setting out (to set out) (PL: wytyczenie, wytyczyc) - transferring dimensions from the drawing onto the ground.
- Mason's string line (PL: sznur murarski) - a thin, strong line stretched between stakes; it defines the line and the level.
- Stake (PL: palik) - a wooden or steel peg driven into the ground as a reference point.
- Spirit level (PL: poziomica, libella) - a tool with an air bubble for checking level and plumb.
- Straightedge (PL: lata) - a straight, rigid batten for checking evenness and screeding.
- Optical/laser level (PL: niwelator) - an instrument for measuring height differences.
- Right angle (PL: kat prosty) - a 90-degree angle; we check it with a square or the 3-4-5 method.

### Tips for the trainer (multilingual group)

- Write numbers and calculations on the whiteboard and also show them physically on the structure (1 m, 2 cm), because numbers are understandable regardless of language.
- Introduce a simple fall conversion table (for example for 1% and 2% over 1 m, 3 m, 5 m) and post it on the welfare-facility wall.
- Use mixed-language pairs, but arrange them so that at least one person in each pair understands the instructions.
- Use gestures: hands showing the inclination, the runoff of water. Repeat the key word three times.
- Hand out the PL/EN/ES materials before the theory block so that the trainees can follow the translation of the terms.

### Common mistakes and how to avoid them

- No fall or a reverse fall (water stands or flows the wrong way): always determine the direction of drainage before setting out and mark it with an arrow.
- Confusing 1% with 1 cm: explain that 1% is 1 cm for every 1 m of length. Practise on specific lengths.
- Crooked angles because the check was skipped: always confirm the right angle with a square or 3-4-5.
- A slack string line (the sag gives a false level): tension the string line firmly and check the level at several points.
- A single measurement with no verification: introduce the iron rule of double-checking every dimension.

## Day 7: Earthworks - topsoil, formation excavation, trench safety, compacting the bottom

### Block header

- Course day: 7 (Week 2, Module 2: Earthworks, base course and subgrade preparation).
- Topic: Stripping topsoil, formation excavation (PL: korytowanie), the depth of the formation, trench safety and underground utilities, manual and machine work, hauling away spoil, compacting the bottom of the formation.
- Duration: about 7 hours of training (excluding breaks).
- Location: active construction site - theory block in the welfare facilities, practical part in the earthworks zone.

### Objectives (knowledge, skills, attitudes)

Knowledge: the trainee understands what topsoil (PL: humus) is and why it is stripped; knows what formation excavation (PL: korytowanie) is and what determines the depth of the formation; knows the basic hazards of excavations and the rules for marking underground utilities; understands the concept of compacting the bottom.

Skills: the trainee can work safely with a shovel and a wheelbarrow, profile the bottom of the formation to a given level, recognise a dangerous excavation and keep a safe distance, and indicate where to haul the spoil.

Attitudes: caution around excavations (a trench is one of the most dangerous places on a site), discipline in keeping to the set depth, care for ergonomics in manual work to protect the spine.

### Materials, tools and PPE

- Materials and aids: stakes and string lines from Day 6 (for depth control), warning tape, utility markings.
- Hand tools: a digging spade and a spoil shovel, a hoe, a rake, a wheelbarrow, a straightedge for checking the evenness of the bottom, a spirit level, a tape.
- Equipment: a plate compactor (PL: zageszczarka, wibroplyta) or a rammer (PL: ubijak, stopa wibracyjna) for the bottom of the formation; for observation, an excavator or mini-excavator operated by an operator.
- PPE: helmet, safety boots with an anti-puncture insole, high-visibility vest, gloves; near the compactor, hearing protectors and glasses; in dust, a dust mask.

### Health and safety issues

- Excavations: we do not enter an excavation with vertical walls deeper than about 1 m without protection (shoring, battering the sides); we keep a safe distance from the edge.
- Underground utilities (electric cables, gas, water, sewerage): before digging we establish their route; if an unidentified conduit is encountered, we stop work and report it.
- Machines: we never stay within the working range of an excavator bucket or in the operator's blind spot; we keep eye contact and use agreed signals.
- Manual handling: load the wheelbarrow sensibly, lift with bent legs, not with the back; do not exceed the lifting limits (roughly up to about 30 kg for men, up to about 12 kg for women in continuous work).
- Stable, dry access routes; remove muddy, slippery spots.

### Course of the lesson

1. Language warm-up and review (about 0.5 h): a review of the concepts of fall and level from Day 6. Words of the day: topsoil, formation, excavation, spoil, compaction, batter (side slope). Showing objects and actions.
2. Theory introduction (about 2 h): the topsoil layer (living, organic, non-load-bearing, which is why it is stripped); formation excavation and the depth of the formation as the sum of the thicknesses of the structural layers (bedding + base course + paving block); compaction of the bottom as the foundation of the whole structure; the safety rules for excavations and utilities. A pavement cross-section diagram on the whiteboard.
3. Trainer demonstration (about 1 h): the trainer shows the correct technique of working with a shovel and loading a wheelbarrow, checking the depth of the formation with a straightedge and string line, and setting up and guiding the compactor on the bottom.
4. Observation of real work on site (about 1 h): from a safe distance the trainees watch the formation excavation and the levelling of the bottom (by hand and by machine), the behaviour of the operator and the banksman, and the way the spoil is hauled away.
5. Supervised hands-on practice (about 2 h): the trainees manually profile a section of the formation bottom to a given depth, check the evenness with a straightedge, load spoil into the wheelbarrow and haul it to the designated place; then, under supervision, they compact the bottom.
6. Discussion of mistakes and wrap-up (about 0.5 h): checking the depth and evenness of the bottom, discussing the ergonomics of manual work, a reminder of the trench safety rules.

### Teaching methods

Step-by-step instruction, demonstration of technique with correction of body posture, guided observation with a list of questions, supervised practice, a short analysis of mistakes on the structure. The emphasis is on safe technique, not on pace.

### Assessment and grading

Observation record: safe technique with a shovel and wheelbarrow, keeping to the set depth of the formation, evenness of the bottom, compliance with trench safety rules and distance from machines. Check questions: why we strip the topsoil, how deep the formation should be for a given structure, what to do after encountering an unknown conduit.

### Key vocabulary

- Topsoil (PL: humus) - the upper, fertile layer of soil; non-load-bearing, removed before construction.
- Formation excavation (PL: korytowanie) - digging out the ground to a given depth to fit the pavement layers.
- Formation (PL: koryto) - the recess created by formation excavation, the bottom for the base course.
- Excavation/trench (PL: wykop) - a recess in the ground; one of the most dangerous places on a site.
- Spoil (PL: urobek) - excavated ground destined to be hauled away.
- Batter / side slope (PL: skarpa) - the inclined wall of an excavation that prevents collapse.
- Compaction (PL: zageszczanie) - ramming the ground to achieve the required bearing capacity and density.
- Utilities (underground) (PL: media podziemne) - cables and pipes (power, gas, water, sewerage) hidden in the ground.
- Levelling (PL: niwelacja) - levelling a surface to the required levels.

### Tips for the trainer (multilingual group)

- Explain trench safety with images and a sharp message; show on a diagram and a sign what the ban on entering an unprotected excavation means.
- Demonstrate the lifting technique on yourself (I bend my knees, straight spine) and ask for it to be repeated; this protects against injuries regardless of language.
- Introduce a clear, simple system of hand signals for machines (stop, back, lift) and practise it.
- Write depths and dimensions as numbers, and physically show the measurement on the formation with a tape.

### Common mistakes and how to avoid them

- A formation that is too deep or too shallow: constantly check the depth with a straightedge and a string line set at the level.
- Leaving pockets of topsoil or organic soil in the bottom: dig everything out down to the load-bearing ground.
- Working manually "with the back" instead of with the legs: correct the posture from the very first wheelbarrow.
- Approaching too close to the excavation edge or to a machine: enforce zones and distance without exception.
- Poor compaction of the bottom (skipped or a single pass): compact evenly, with overlapping passes.

## Day 8: Base course - structural layers, profiling, compacting in layers

### Block header

- Course day: 8 (Week 2, Module 2: Earthworks, base course and subgrade preparation).
- Topic: The filter/drainage layer and the main base course, crushed aggregates, layer thicknesses, placing and profiling, compacting in layers and the compaction index, the role of water, geotextile and separation.
- Duration: about 7 hours of training (excluding breaks).
- Location: active construction site - theory block in the welfare facilities, practical part on a section of base course being prepared.

### Objectives (knowledge, skills, attitudes)

Knowledge: the trainee understands the arrangement of the structural pavement layers and the role of each of them; knows why we compact in layers rather than the whole thickness at once; understands what the geotextile is for and the role that water plays during compaction.

Skills: the trainee can spread and level out aggregate to a given thickness, profile a layer with a fall, correctly guide the compactor with overlapping passes, and lay geotextile with an overlap.

Attitudes: patience (a good base course is work layer by layer), care for even compaction, awareness that this "invisible" layer decides the durability.

### Materials, tools and PPE

- Materials: crushed aggregate for the base course (for example chippings, broken stone, a 0-31.5 mm mix), aggregate for the filter/drainage layer (for example sand, all-in aggregate), geotextile, water for wetting.
- Tools: shovels, rakes, wheelbarrows, a profiling straightedge, string lines and stakes, a spirit level, a tape, a checking straightedge.
- Equipment: a plate compactor (PL: zageszczarka, wibroplyta), a rammer if needed; a hose or watering can for wetting.
- PPE: helmet, safety boots, vest, gloves, hearing protectors and glasses near the compactor, a dust mask with dry, dusty aggregate.

### Health and safety issues

- Compactor: work with hearing protectors and glasses; a firm grip, feet away from the plate; we do not leave a running machine unattended; transport the compactor with two people or on a trolley.
- Dust from dry aggregate: a dust mask, wetting with water reduces dust.
- Manual transport of aggregate: ergonomics, sensible loading of the wheelbarrow, even access routes.
- Slippery geotextile and a wet layer: watch out for slipping; laid geotextile can be slippery before it is covered.
- Vibration from the compactor: rotate people, take breaks, do not extend continuous work beyond a reasonable time.

### Course of the lesson

1. Language warm-up and review (about 0.5 h): a review of the formation bottom and compaction from Day 7. Words of the day: layer, base course, crushed aggregate, profiling, compactor, geotextile, compaction index.
2. Theory introduction (about 2 h): the pavement cross-section layer by layer (natural ground, geotextile, filter/drainage layer, main base course, bedding layer, paving block); the functions of the layers; the principle of compacting in layers (for example layers of about 15-20 cm), overlapping of the compactor passes by about 10-20 cm; the compaction index as a measure of quality; the role of water (the right moisture makes compaction easier); the separating role of the geotextile.
3. Trainer demonstration (about 1 h): the trainer lays the geotextile with an overlap, spreads and profiles a layer with a straightedge to a given thickness with a fall, shows model guiding of the compactor (direction, overlapping of passes, number of passes).
4. Observation of real work on site (about 1 h): the trainees watch the crew placing and compacting the base course, measuring the layer thicknesses and checking compaction, if it is carried out on site.
5. Supervised hands-on practice (about 2 h): the trainees lay the geotextile, place and profile a layer of aggregate to a given thickness with a fall, wet it with water and compact it, checking evenness with a straightedge.
6. Discussion of mistakes and wrap-up (about 0.5 h): checking the thickness, evenness and fall of the layer; discussing the correct pattern of compactor passes; a reminder of the rule "in layers, not the whole thickness at once".

### Teaching methods

Visualisation of the cross-section (a drawing and a real exposed section), demonstration of technique, supervised practice with rotation at the compactor, guided observation, analysis of the result (evenness, compaction) directly on the layer that has been completed.

### Assessment and grading

Observation record: correct thickness and evenness of the layer, the fall maintained, correct overlapping of the compactor passes, correct laying of the geotextile with an overlap. Check questions: why we compact in layers, what the geotextile is for, what the role of water is.

### Key vocabulary

- Base course (PL: podbudowa) - the load-bearing aggregate layer beneath the bedding and the paving block.
- Filter/drainage layer (PL: warstwa odsaczajaca) - the lower layer that carries water away from the structure.
- Crushed aggregate (PL: kruszywo lamane) - aggregate with sharp edges (chippings, broken stone) that interlocks and compacts well.
- Profiling (PL: profilowanie) - giving a layer an even surface and the correct fall.
- Compactor (plate compactor) (PL: zageszczarka, wibroplyta) - a machine that rams aggregate with vibrations.
- Compaction index (PL: wskaznik zageszczenia) - a numerical measure of how well a layer has been compacted.
- Geotextile (PL: geowloknina) - a technical fabric that separates layers and stabilises the subgrade.
- Overlap (PL: zakladka) - the overlapping of adjacent strips of geotextile on each other.

### Tips for the trainer (multilingual group)

- Show an exposed section or a cross-sectional model of the layers; the image of a layer "cake" is understandable without words.
- Draw the compaction pattern (overlapping passes) with arrows on the ground or on the whiteboard and physically walk the path so that the trainees copy the movement.
- Write the layer thicknesses as numbers and show them with a tape; write the 15-20 cm range unambiguously.
- Rotate people at the compactor, making sure everyone understands how to start and stop the machine.

### Common mistakes and how to avoid them

- Compacting too thick a layer at once (the bottom stays loose): keep to the maximum layer thickness and compact each one separately.
- No overlap of the compactor passes (loose strips between passes): enforce an overlap of about 10-20 cm.
- Aggregate that is too dry or too wet: maintain the optimum moisture; wet moderately.
- Geotextile with no overlap or wrinkled: lay it evenly, with an overlap, and tension it.
- Skipping the fall in the base course (it is then hard to restore in the bedding): profile the fall already in the base course.

## Day 9: Edge restraints and kerbs - concrete bed, seating in line and level

### Block header

- Course day: 9 (Week 2, Module 2: Earthworks, base course and subgrade preparation).
- Topic: A concrete bed with a haunch, seating the kerb and edge restraint in line and level along the string line, joints, curves and corners, concreting the bed.
- Duration: about 7 hours of training (excluding breaks).
- Location: active construction site - theory block in the welfare facilities, practical part along the line of edge restraints and kerbs.

### Objectives (knowledge, skills, attitudes)

Knowledge: the trainee understands the role of edge restraints and kerbs (closing off and stiffening the pavement); knows what a concrete bed with a haunch is and why it is needed; understands the importance of the string line as the line and the level.

Skills: the trainee can prepare mortar or concrete for the bed, seat a kerb and an edge restraint in a single line and a single level along the string line, keep even joints, form a simple curve and a corner, and tap the unit home with a rubber mallet without damaging it.

Attitudes: precision (a crooked kerb is visible from afar and spoils the whole pavement), patience when setting to the string line, care for the cleanliness of the units.

### Materials, tools and PPE

- Materials: concrete kerbs and edge restraints, concrete or mortar for the bed (for example a cement-aggregate mix), cement, water, aggregate, string line, stakes.
- Tools: a trowel, a float, a rubber mallet, a shovel, a wheelbarrow or a container for mixing, a spirit level, a straightedge, a tape, a square, a brush.
- Equipment: a concrete mixer or paddle mixer if needed; for observation, the crew working along the kerb line.
- PPE: helmet, safety boots, vest, gloves resistant to mortar (cement is irritant and can cause chemical burns), glasses when mixing and cutting, a mask if needed.

### Health and safety issues

- Cement and fresh mortar are caustic: we protect the skin (gloves, long sleeves) and the eyes; after contact we rinse with water.
- Heavy kerbs: we lift with two people or using a clamp, with leg technique, not with the back.
- Rubber mallet: a firm grip, watch the fingers when tapping the unit home.
- Slippery, fresh concrete bed: we mark the zone so that nobody steps onto it.
- Tidiness: spilled mortar is cleaned up as we go so there are no slips.

### Course of the lesson

1. Language warm-up and review (about 0.5 h): a review of the base course layers from Day 8. Words of the day: kerb, edge restraint, concrete bed, haunch, joint, in line, in level, corner, curve.
2. Theory introduction (about 2 h): the function of edge restraints and kerbs; the construction of a concrete bed with a haunch (support from the outer side); the principle of seating to a string line that defines the line and the top of the unit; the width and evenness of joints; how to form a curve (shorter joints on the inner side) and a corner; the proportions and consistency of the concrete for the bed.
3. Trainer demonstration (about 1 h): the trainer stretches a string line, places a portion of concrete, seats a kerb on the bed, taps it home with a rubber mallet to the string line in line and level, forms the haunch, sets an even joint, cleans the unit.
4. Observation of real work on site (about 1 h): the trainees watch the crew seating kerbs on the bed, checking line and level, and forming a curve or a corner.
5. Supervised hands-on practice (about 2 h): the trainees prepare the concrete for the bed, seat several kerbs or edge restraints along the string line in line and level, form the haunch, set even joints; volunteers try a simple curve.
6. Discussion of mistakes and wrap-up (about 0.5 h): checking the line, level and joints against the string line and the spirit level; discussing corrections; a reminder about the haunch and cleanliness.

### Teaching methods

A master's demonstration with explanation, supervised practice on a real section, mutual checking in pairs (one lays, the other checks the string line and level), analysis of the result on the structure.

### Assessment and grading

Observation record: seating in a single line and a single level along the string line, a correctly formed bed haunch, even joints, a clean unit, observance of safety around cement. Check questions: what the haunch is for, how to set the unit to the string line, how to make a joint on a curve.

### Key vocabulary

- Kerb (PL: kraweznik) - a concrete unit bounding a pavement, usually higher (next to a carriageway).
- Edge restraint (PL: obrzeze) - a lower unit framing a footpath or paved area.
- Concrete bed (PL: lawa betonowa) - the concrete base on which the kerb or edge restraint is seated.
- Haunch (PL: opor) - additional concrete supporting the unit from the outer side.
- Joint (PL: spoina) - the gap between adjacent units.
- In line (PL: w linii) - set straight along the string line.
- In level (PL: w poziomie) - set with the correct top according to the string line and the spirit level.
- Corner (PL: narozze) - the point where the line bends at an angle.
- Curve (PL: luk) - a gentle rounding of the kerb line.

### Tips for the trainer (multilingual group)

- Emphasise the role of the string line visually: show how the unit "touches" the string line at a single point (line and top). This is clear without words.
- Explain cement safety with a pictogram of a caustic substance and a hand-washing demonstration; enforce gloves.
- Introduce roles in the pair: "layer" and "string-line checker", swapping over so that everyone practises both tasks.
- Show the joints with a gauge (for example a spacer that matches the design) and point out that they must be even.

### Common mistakes and how to avoid them

- A unit below or above the string line (a wavy line of the top): tap exactly to the string line, check with the spirit level.
- No haunch on the bed (the kerb tilts over time): always form the haunch on the outer side.
- Uneven joints: use a spacer or a gauge and check every joint.
- Concrete for the bed that is too wet or too dry: watch the consistency so the unit does not settle and can be set.
- Dirty units flooded with mortar: clean as you go, before the mortar hardens.

## Day 10: Bedding layer, weekly review and Partial Test no. 2

### Block header

- Course day: 10 (Week 2, Module 2: Earthworks, base course and subgrade preparation).
- Topic: Types of bedding layer (cement-sand about 1:4, sand), thickness of 3-5 cm after compaction, screed rails and spacer pipes, screeding with a straightedge, evenness, the rule of not walking on the finished bedding; review of the whole week and Partial Test no. 2.
- Duration: about 7 hours of training (excluding breaks).
- Location: active construction site - theory block in the welfare facilities, practical part on a section of bedding being prepared.

### Objectives (knowledge, skills, attitudes)

Knowledge: the trainee understands the role of the bedding layer as the levelling course beneath the paving block; knows the types and proportions (cement-sand about 1:4); knows that the thickness after compaction is about 3-5 cm; understands the rule of not walking on the finished bedding. Review: the trainee consolidates the material of the whole of Week 2.

Skills: the trainee can prepare the bedding, set up the screed rails (spacer pipes), screed an even surface with a fall using a straightedge, and work "from the face" without stepping onto the finished bedding.

Attitudes: precision and cleanliness of the layer directly beneath the paving block, discipline in working "from the face", systematic effort in the review and conscientiousness during the test.

### Materials, tools and PPE

- Materials: sand (or sand with cement in a proportion of about 1:4 for cement-sand bedding), cement, water; spacer pipes (screed rails), the Partial Test no. 2 sheet in the language versions.
- Tools: shovels, rakes, a screeding straightedge, a spirit level, a tape, a trowel, a wheelbarrow or a container for mixing, string lines and stakes.
- Equipment: a compactor (if the bedding is compacted according to the technology), a concrete mixer or paddle mixer if needed.
- PPE: helmet, safety boots, vest, gloves (mortar-resistant near cement), glasses and hearing protectors when compacting, a mask in dust; knee pads when working on the knees.

### Health and safety issues

- Cement in cement-sand bedding: protection of the skin and eyes, as on Day 9.
- Working on the knees while screeding: knee pads, changing position to protect the joints.
- Dust from sand and cement: a mask, wetting to limit dust.
- Compactor (if used): hearing protectors, glasses, a safe grip.
- The test: calm conditions, but constant observance of the safety rules of the working zone during the practical part.

### Course of the lesson

1. Language warm-up and weekly review (about 1 h): a quick review of the whole of Week 2 (falls and levels, earthworks, base course, kerbs) in the form of questions and showing objects. Words of the day: bedding, screed rails, spacer pipes, screeding with a straightedge, from the face.
2. Theory introduction (about 1.5 h): the role and types of bedding; the cement-sand proportion of about 1:4; thickness of 3-5 cm after compaction; the method of screed rails (pipes) and screeding with a straightedge; the absolute rule of not walking on the finished, screeded bedding and of working "from the face".
3. Trainer demonstration (about 0.75 h): the trainer lays the spacer pipes to a given thickness, spreads the bedding, screeds with a straightedge along the rails to obtain an even surface with a fall, removes the pipes and floats over the marks, shows entering "from the face".
4. Observation of real work on site (about 0.75 h): the trainees watch the crew preparing and screeding the bedding just before laying the paving blocks, paying attention to the fact that nobody treads on the finished layer.
5. Supervised hands-on practice (about 1.25 h): the trainees set up the screed rails, spread the bedding and screed an even surface with a fall using a straightedge, practising working "from the face"; checking evenness with a straightedge and a spirit level.
6. Partial Test no. 2 and discussion (about 1 h): the written Partial Test no. 2 covering the Week 2 material (about 0.5 h), then a joint discussion of the answers and a wrap-up of the module (about 0.5 h), including a short discussion of mistakes from the bedding exercise.

### Teaching methods

Activating review (question and showing), demonstration of technique with the screed rails, supervised practice, a written test in the language versions with the option of translation support for understanding the instructions, discussion of the results as feedback.

### Assessment and grading

Partial Test no. 2 (written): a pass according to the set threshold; it covers falls, earthworks, the base course, kerbs and the bedding. Practical-observation record: correct thickness and evenness of the bedding, the fall maintained, working "from the face", not treading on the finished layer. The test result and the practical grade count towards the condition for completing the course.

### Key vocabulary

- Bedding (PL: podsypka) - a thin layer (sand or sand with cement) directly beneath the paving block; it levels the subgrade.
- Cement-sand (PL: cementowo-piaskowa) - bedding from a mix of sand and cement (roughly 1:4).
- Screed rails (spacer pipes) (PL: prowadnice, rury dystansowe) - pipes laid in parallel, along which the straightedge is screeded to a given thickness.
- Screeding with a straightedge (PL: sciaganie lata) - levelling the bedding with a straight batten slid along the screed rails.
- From the face (PL: z czola) - working from the side of the already laid surface, without stepping onto the fresh bedding.
- Evenness (PL: rownosc) - the absence of surface irregularities, checked with a straightedge and a spirit level.

### Tips for the trainer (multilingual group)

- Reinforce the rule "we do not walk on the finished bedding" with a prohibition sign and a demonstration; this is a common and costly breach, so enforce it firmly.
- Show the screed-rail method physically: laying the pipes, screeding with the straightedge, removing the pipes. Movement is remembered better than a description.
- Prepare the test in PL/EN/ES versions with simple sentences, pictograms and single-choice questions; provide translation support for understanding the instructions (not for prompting the answers).
- After the test, go calmly through every question, explaining the correct answer; this closes off the week's learning.

### Common mistakes and how to avoid them

- Stepping onto the finished bedding (footprints, local depressions): work only "from the face", enforce the no-entry rule.
- Wrong bedding thickness (too thick is then compensated by tapping the block down, which spoils the evenness): keep to 3-5 cm after compaction according to the screed rails.
- Screeding without maintaining the fall: set the screed rails with a fall and check with the spirit level.
- Marks left by the pipes without floating over: remove the pipes and gently fill and float over the grooves.
- Treating the test as a formality: explain that the result is a condition for completion, and link the questions to daily practice on the site.
